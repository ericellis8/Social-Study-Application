A League Of Their Own

Eric Ellis

I’ve never seen anything quite like it before, but the motion picture, “A League of their Own” is just a great movie all around.  It tells the story of an all girls baseball league that was formed during World War II.  Most of the professional baseball players had stopped playing in order to go fight for their country overseas. This left a huge hole in the baseball world and they needed to fulfill it somehow.  Their solution was to get the best softball players from around the country to come play professional baseball.  They called it the “All American Girls Professional Baseball League.”  It wasn’t easy and just about didn’t work initially, but it turned into a huge success.

In the reading from week 2, “The Rise and Decline of Organized Women’s Sports, 1980-1960,” it discusses the “All American Girls Professional Baseball League” and lines up pretty accurately to how to the film portrayed it.  The reading talks how they were required to wear short dresses and attend a charm school to learn how to apply makeup, fix their hair, and display proper feminine social graces.  This is exactly what was shown in the movie.  The women were initially opposed to the idea of having to play in short dresses but quickly accepted it once they were told to either wear them or not play.

Wearing dresses while playing sports for women was very common in the beginning of sport for women.  Although, wearing a short dress was fairly new at the time.  Women were supposed to wear dresses that covered up all parts of their body as to not make their sport sexual.  

There were a few historical inaccuracies in this film such as the man who initiated the league was a candy bar mogul.  When in fact, the owner of Wrigley Gum created it.  They probably just didn’t have permission to use his name in the movie.  Along with that, during the tryouts they called the field some random name, where it was actually Wrigley field, home of the Chicago Cubs.  Also, all of the characters in the movie were fictional, with only a couple that were loosely based off of real women (AAGPBL.com).

The Real AAGPBL

In the movie, at the end of tryouts they posted the rosters for the four teams on a wall near the dugout and everyone ran over to see if they had made it.  After reading the league history from the official website of the AAGPBL, it actually states that they found out if they made the team by telephone.  They depict a girl looking at the list and crying, not because she was cut, but because she couldn’t read. While it may have been the case that one of the players couldn’t read, this event never happened in real life.

Something that wasn’t discussed entirely during the film was the rules of the game.  They made it appear that the women were playing the exact same game as the men played.  While most of that is true, they did not play on same size fields.  Base paths ranged from 65 feet early in the league to 85 feet in the last season. Men, on the other hand, played on base paths with a length of 90 feet.  The pitching mound was also closer than the men’s, with the exception of the last year of the league, where it was moved back to 60 feet.  It’s interesting to note that in the movie they always pitched overhand.  But really, they pitched underhand for the first four years of the league.  They eventually changed to overhand, but the movie took place during the first season of the league.  

There wasn’t much discussion of the women’s athletic career before they were in the AAGPBL in the movie. They didn’t give much insight to where these girls came from.  An article of the AAGPBL website talks about Jean Ann and her athletic career growing up.  She was a shortstop for the Fort Wayne Daises during the 1953 and 1954 seasons.  When she was a child, she played football at the local playground and would tuck her pigtails into her helmet in order to do so.  That was until an opposing team found out she was a girl and was actually banned from playing football. She was such a talented athlete she also participated in hockey, baseball, basketball, and competed as a speed skater. After her time in the AAGPBL, she went on to compete on a national level as a bowler.  She is in the Women’s International Bowling Congress Hall of Fame, as well as the only bowler in the Minnesota Sports Hall of Fame.

Many of the baseball players were just like Ann, not only very good at baseball, but excelled it other sports as well.  The movie really just focused on the baseball aspects and never got into too much detail of their other athletic prowess.

The Readings, AAGPBL Website, and A League Of Their Own

One thing that was constant throughout the readings, the league website, and the motion picture for theses athletes was the need to act like a “lady.” From Jennifer Hargreaves “the Victorian cult of the family and the early years of female sport” she states that, “women were obliged to show restraint, be refined and respectable, and confirm at all times the ‘ladylike’ modes of behavior prescribed for them.” In the movie, as well as described from their website, the women were act proper at all times.  Smoking was not allowed, nor was drinking.  Some players apposed to the strict rules they had to live by and even broke them occasionally, but they adhered to them in order to stay in the league.

Hargreaves also states that “The relationship between the man and the woman was viewed as a reciprocal one in that the woman’s dependent role as wife, housekeeper, and child bearer, which confined her to ‘the inferior world of the familyâ€¦ left the bourgeois man free to accumulate capital’ in order to maintain her adequately.”  I feel like this was shown at the end of the movie when Dottie’s husband returns home from war and she immediately packed up to return home.  What she was doing at the time was not nearly as important as being a housewife for her husband.  

Another part of the movie that was true is the fact that no African American women participated in the league.  They didn’t address this much in the movie but there was a brief clip of a foul ball landing near some African American’s in the crowd.  One of the girls picked up the ball and through it back in extremely hard and everyone looked impressed.  It wasn’t talked about but it was easy to tell they were not allowed to play.  In the article by Rader, it states, “that the league recruited no black players reflected not only the racism of the day but the prevailing white stereotypes of female beauty as well.”

Rader also states “while industrial basketball exploited the sexual attractions of women and the dominance of male coaches limited the chances for women professionals in these fields, the female athletes themselves rarely objected.”  This was very prevalent throughout the movie, being forced to wear short dresses and being coached by males.  The players were even asked to make a good pose while playing the game for the photographers to capture a picture.  While originally opposed to the idea, Dottie eventually noticed that the reporters were losing interest, so on a foul bowl hit behind home plate; she did the splits as she caught the ball. This grabbed the attention of everyone and appeared to completely change the way people thought of them playing baseball.

 It was quite an accomplishment of what the players of the All American Girls Professional Baseball League where able to do.  Being able to play a male’s sport and attract fans to the games with their amount of success just hasn’t really been seen since then.  They faced many challenges along the way, had to adhere to strict rules that would never be forced on men, and still had to play the sport at a high level.  I think it was great for the advancement of women in sports as well as just a great opportunity for those athletes that participated.  If there were a similar situation today, women from all over the world would be jumping at the opportunity.
